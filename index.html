TYPES OF MATERIALS

Ferromagnetic materials

Definition: Strong magnetic materials that retain magnetization.


Key Characteristics: High magnetic permeability. Strong attraction to magnetic fields. Exhibit a hysteresis loop. Retain magnetization even after the external field is removed.

Examples: Iron, Cobalt, Nickel.

Applications: Permanent magnets, Transformers, Magnetic storage devices.
.

Paramagnetic materials

Definition: Weakly attracted to magnetic fields, do not retain magnetization.


Characteristics: Positive but small magnetic susceptibility. Alignment of magnetic dipoles only in the presence of an external field.

Examples: Aluminum, Platinum, Magnesium.

Applications: Used in scientific instruments and materials research.

Diamagnetic materials

Definition: Weakly repelled by magnetic fields.


Characteristics: Negative magnetic susceptibility. Oppose the external magnetic field slightly. Magnetic effect is very weak compared to paramagnetic and ferromagnetic materials.

Examples: Copper, Gold, Bismuth.

Applications: Magnetic levitation (e.g., diamagnetic levitation of graphite), Magnetic shielding.

Ferrimagnetic materials

Definition: Similar to antiferromagnetic materials, but the opposing magnetic moments are unequal, resulting in a net magnetic moment.
Characteristics:

Definition: Materials in which adjacent magnetic dipoles align in opposite directions, canceling out the net magnetic moment.

Superparamagnetic materials

Definition: Nanostructured materials that exhibit paramagnetic-like behavior but with stronger magnetic moments.
Characteristics: No hysteresis or residual magnetization, Strongly influenced by external fields.
Examples: Iron oxide nanoparticles, Cobalt nanoparticles.
Applications: Biomedical imaging (e.g., MRI contrast agents), Magnetic storage systems.

Superconducting materials

Definition: Materials that completely expel magnetic fields below a critical temperature (Meissner effect).
Characteristics: Zero electrical resistance, Perfect diamagnetism.
Examples: Yttrium barium copper oxide (YBCO), Niobium, Lead.
Applications: Magnetic levitation (maglev trains) Particle accelerators.

LOOPS

Solenoid
A solenoid is a coil of wire that creates a magnetic field when an electric current passes through it. It is typically used to convert electrical energy into mechanical movement.
Toroid
A coil of wire wound into a ring-shaped (doughnut-shaped) core. It is similar to a solenoid but bent into a circular shape.
Straight current carrying wire
A long, straight wire carrying current produces a circular magnetic field around it. The direction of the field follows the right-hand rule.
Loop of Current
A circular loop of current generates a magnetic field similar to that of a small bar magnet. The field is strongest at the center of the loop.
